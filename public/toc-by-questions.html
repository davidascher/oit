Tool picking by asking “big questions”

A natural language way to access the content in the Innovation Toolkit

How can I validate my assumptions about this feature quickly (and without expert help)?

  • Do you have the right feature? Check out our research section for a few simple ways to engage friends, colleagues, family, strangers in productive conversation.
  • Will your implementation of the feature meet user needs? Best to prototype rapidly. Check out our prototype section, especially paper prototypes and storyboarding

I am interested in a particular user problem, how do I get started in creating a solution?

  • First, make sure you understand the problem you want to solve by talking to people and observing their behaviors. Check out our research section, especially Interviewing 101 and Guerilla Research
  • Grab a few colleagues and practice divergent thinking to come up with a novel solution: Check out our workshops section, especially Feature Ideation / User Need Workshop.

What are the methods to run an ideation session to get new thinking on my product?

  • Check out our workshops section, especially Idea Generation and Participatory Design/Co-design Worksession.
  • Feel free to consult with the Innovation Team to get additional tips tuned to your particular situation and product.

How can I create a meaningful prototype without coding?

Maximum return/minimum effort! Try quick light-weight tests to make sure you’re on the right path: In the prototype section, focus especially on paper prototypes and Click-through / interactive prototype.

How can I uncover problems? ("I have this solution, tell me what the right problem is for it")

  • We need to shift our mindset at Mozilla to understand that technology-first solutions are inherently limited. By developing empathy for our users (not just makers!), building solutions for real-world non-frivolous problems, and striving to create meaningful value propositions not just technical achievements, we will be able to make enormous positive impact on people’s lives.
  • In terms of employing specific methods, focus on surfacing insights of real people by making observations and asking questions. Check out the research section, especially Interviewing 101 and Guerilla Research.

How can we build user feedback loops into our development process so we can learn and iterate quickly?

As part of the innovation process currently applied in Connected Devices “Trains,” we are encouraging the creation of meaningful “milestone” checkins based on the product roadmap and MVP plan. The team should extract milestones every 2-4 weeks.

Milestones are measurable activities in several categories: user engagement, operations, engineering, communication. (Continuous user engagement is critical to product success and needs to be treated on par with other management practices.) Examples of milestones include: onboard new mobile engineer, share learnings on implementation Y with other teams, interview # experts, release feature X, dogfood in Z location.

These milestones help act as guideposts of progress and are the core to communication around progress between the team and the Product Innovation Board. The milestones are not just way to check in on the team’s progress, but they give the board and PM a chance to help the teams through resourcing, introductions, and other support.

How do we prototype, workshop, and innovate while being remote?

Remote teams need to work harder to be in sync, yet fight off the tendency to over-schedule meetings. The best advice for teams working remotely on innovation processes is to establish habit and focus on communication.

Habit: daily standups and monthly face-to-face meetings; all member engage with real world users (X# of interviews/observations/brainstorms a week).

Communication: structuring contributions so that people share learnings not just accomplishments.

How can I size a target market?

Generally there are two ways of defining a target market size: bottom up and top down. Bottom up is considered a more reliable method and one we recommend also. Bottom up approach requires you first to define your target customer and then estimating how many of them you can reach, and how many of the ones you can reach will buy your product. More info: Steve Blank, Wall Street Journal and MIT entrepreneur class 101.

What is a good definition of a target customer?

Identifying your target customer is about “finding out who the customers for your product are and whether the problem you believe you are solving is important to them” - Steve Blank. We do this by defining not just the type of person, but the problem we are solving and why the target customer is interested in having that problem solved. More info: Steve Blank (p30).
